Kieran Hebden has a problem. For years, he's been releasing music under an alias so cryptic that most platforms can't even display it consistently. The name, a jumble of Unicode characters and combining diacritics, renders differently on YouTube Music, Apple Music, Bandcamp, and Spotify. It's the kind of technical nightmare that makes you wonder: if an artist releases music under a name nobody can read, did they really release it at all?

This is the story of Four Tet's secret project, now officially known as "Wingdings" after gaining millions of streams. And it's a fascinating window into how fragmented the digital music landscape really is.

Hebden's decision to use an unrenderable artist name is either genius or an elaborate prank. Probably both. For a project that's been quietly accumulating millions of listens, the album remained shrouded in mystery. Subscriber morgannels eventually brought it to wider attention through Infeeds' community spaces, but how many people never found it simply because they couldn't search for it? How many streaming algorithms treated each rendering as a separate artist?

This raises uncomfortable questions about [music discovery](https://infeeds.com/tags/music) in 2024. We've built these massive platforms designed to connect artists with listeners, yet a deliberate choice to use non-standard Unicode completely breaks that system. The platforms handle it differently, prioritize it differently, and represent it differently to users.

## The Music Itself: Worth the Hunt

But here's the thing: the album is genuinely excellent. The tracks lean into Four Tet's dancier sensibilities without sacrificing the thoughtfulness that's always defined his work. These aren't the throwaway B-sides you might expect from a secret dumping ground. They're polished, infectious, and deeply groovy.

Track two features deep four-on-the-floor house drums paired with climbing arpeggios that feel infinite. Track three blends metallic gamelan-inspired melodies with ultradeep EDM wobbles. It's the kind of production work that rewards close listening, whether you're focused on it or just letting it soundtrack your day.

What's particularly striking is how Wingdings balances danceability with introspection. These aren't high-octane rave tracks designed to destroy a dance floor. Instead, songs build layered textures that feel spiritual rather than purely recreational. Track seven climaxes with such careful attention to detail that you can almost feel the emotional weight climbing. Track eight is pure ambient comedown, beautiful and necessary.

## The Fragmentation Problem

The real issue here isn't about Four Tet's artistic choices. It's about how [streaming services](https://infeeds.com/tags/tech) have fundamentally fragmented music distribution. An album that's available on Bandcamp, Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music, Deezer, and Qobuz isn't really available everywhere in a meaningful way.

Each platform renders the artist name differently. Each one has different recommendation algorithms, different metadata handling, different audio quality options. From a listener's perspective, they're not accessing the same album in six places. They're accessing six similar but distinct versions of it.

Hebden made an artistic choice that technically works within these systems while also exposing their limitations. The unpronounceable name is a mirror held up to the weird, fragmented reality of modern music distribution. Streaming was supposed to solve everything. Instead, we got convenient access coupled with profound standardization problems.

So go listen to Wingdings. Hunt for it under the glyphs or find it under Four Tet's name. Notice how it sounds different depending on where you stream it. Appreciate the music itself, which absolutely deserves that appreciation. And then sit with the uncomfortable reality that we're relying on a handful of platforms to mediate our access to art, and those platforms aren't even equipped to handle what artists want to do with their own work.
