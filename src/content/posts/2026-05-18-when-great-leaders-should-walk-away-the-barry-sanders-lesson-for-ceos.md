Barry Sanders was 31 years old and still the best running back in football. He was also 1,457 yards away from breaking the all-time rushing record. He had just put up 1,491 yards the previous season. Experts figured he had four or five solid years left.

He retired anyway.

Most people called it irrational. Sanders had already won every award that mattered. He'd made his money. The record was within reach. But he walked away because staying would have cost him something he valued more: his health, his humility, and how people would remember him.

That decision, made in 1998, offers one of the clearest lessons in modern <a href="https://infeeds.com/tags/?tag=business">business</a> thinking. Not for athletes, but for executives who spend decades wrestling with the same question: when is it actually time to go?

## The Peak vs. the Scoreboard

Most CEOs confuse longevity with achievement. They stay in the role until the board suggests they shouldn't, until the stock price stalls, until succession becomes an emergency rather than a plan. Sanders refused that trade.

"A CEO should step down when the role no longer demands their full capacity, and that usually happens before the scoreboard reflects it," the thinking goes. Sanders knew he was still performing at an elite level. That was precisely why he left.

This creates a paradox that makes executives deeply uncomfortable. The moment you're most qualified to lead is often the moment you should consider stepping away. Not because you're declining, but because you're approaching the point where you might.

One founder built a company from $500 million to $2.7 billion over four years. Profits increased nineteenfold. When the private equity firm sold it, the returns landed in the 99th percentile of their historical performance. That was the best season any CEO could reasonably ask for. Continuing past it would have been the exact trade Sanders refused to make.

## Succession Planning Isn't Something You Do When You Leave

The difference between a strategic exit and just quitting comes down to architecture. One has a timeline, an identified successor, and a bench developed before the moment becomes urgent. The other is a departure that leaves the organization absorbing shock for the next eighteen months.

Sanders' retirement looked sudden to the public. Internally, he had been preparing for it for years. The same approach applies to CEO transitions.

This means the clock doesn't start when you decide to leave. It starts the day you take the role. Private equity environments understand this instinctively. Every day is day one of planning for the transition.

Actual quitting looks different. It's the absence of succession architecture. It's the CEO who overstays, the sudden departure with no plan, the exit so abrupt that the organization staggers through a leadership vacuum. The distinction isn't emotional. It's structural, and it becomes visible the moment the transition is announced.

## Identity is the Real Trap

At dinner parties, people ask executives what they do. Most trained themselves to answer with their title. For many leaders, losing the role begins to feel like losing themselves.

This is where ego enters the conversation, not as naked ambition but as something quieter and harder to see. A CEO's children might prefer the version where their parent runs something. A board might seem eager to create a chairman role. The opportunity to stay involved in some capacity almost always presents itself.

But here's what separates leaders who preserve their legacy from those who diminish it: the ability to separate identity from title.

Sanders' identity was being the best running back alive, not simply being an active one. Once he wasn't performing at that level anymore, the role itself became less interesting to him than the memory he would leave behind.

The financial side of stepping away is straightforward math. Math rarely creates internal conflict. Ego does.

## The Diminished Role Trap

Transitioning from CEO to chairman works only when the new role genuinely benefits from the former executive's experience. It fails spectacularly when the position exists mainly to preserve proximity to power.

The difference becomes obvious quickly to everyone except the person in the role.

One executive made a mistake by remaining on the board after stepping down as CEO. Without intending to, they influenced discussions in ways that complicated the incoming CEO's strategy. Institutional memory doesn't disappear when a title changes. It gets corrected eventually, but not before creating friction that shouldn't exist.

Since then, this person has not remained on the board of a company they previously ran. The lesson was clear: no new CEO truly wants the former CEO hovering nearby. The leaders who insist they're comfortable with it are often the ones who most need the separation.

There's also a quieter danger in accepting smaller roles well into your seventies. You watch the scope of those roles gradually shrink around you. A great operator leading a diminished mandate eventually appears diminished themselves. The stature built during your final operating role gets slowly reduced, year after year.

Sanders never accepted a diminished role. He stepped away entirely, which is one reason the image of him at his peak survived intact. A better alternative is often stepping away from a single operating seat and working across multiple companies in advisory or investment capacities instead. That preserves the stature you built rather than eroding it.

## Three Tests for the Sanders Moment

Recognizing when it's actually time requires honest assessment across three dimensions:

The capacity test asks whether the role still demands your full capacity. If the annual plan writes itself and the board has stopped pushing back, you might already have your answer.

The succession test is whether the next leader is identified, developed, and genuinely ready. If the transition requires more than six months of runway, the process probably started too late.

The identity test is the hardest one. Is the title your activity or your identity? If losing the title feels like losing yourself, the attachment itself becomes the risk.

Executives who can answer these questions honestly tend to leave with their legacy intact. The ones who cannot often become cautionary examples that successors reference quietly behind closed doors.

Plan the exit with the same rigor you used to plan the ascent, and the legacy usually takes care of itself.
