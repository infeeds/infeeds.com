The adrenaline of a crisis fix is intoxicating. A system crashes, you swoop in, diagnose the problem in minutes, and save the day. Your team looks relieved. Your boss notices. You feel essential.

This is the fixer trap, and it's disguised as competence.

Most of us don't recognize it as a trap at all. We see it as leadership. We see it as being reliable, competent, and results-driven. What we don't see is the slow erosion of our team's confidence, the atrophy of their problem-solving skills, and the invisible ceiling we've placed on organizational growth.

## The Heroic Problem Solver Isn't Actually Leading

Early in a tech-driven career, the urge to fix things is almost irresistible. You're good at it. You move fast. You thrive on pressure. Every time you step in and resolve a crisis, it validates your presence and, more seductively, it makes your team depend on you.

One moment crystallizes this pattern. During a game day with the Clippers, a major systems failure knocked out premium suite access. Using binary troubleshooting (testing extremes rather than incrementally guessing), the problem was diagnosed and fixed before halftime. Another win. Another hero moment.

Then one of the top engineers casually said, "You know I could have figured that out, right?"

The tone wasn't frustrated. It was measured, almost sad. And that's when the real damage became visible. It wasn't the fix itself. It was the message underneath: I didn't trust you to solve this. In the weeks that followed, her curiosity faded. She stopped raising her hand in meetings. The opportunity for growth had been stolen.

The same pattern showed up later at a talent agency during a platform rollout. Adoption was slow, so the instinct kicked in: step in as the bridge between technical and creative teams, translate problems, smooth friction, accelerate progress. It worked. Things moved faster when involvement increased.

Until they didn't. When not available, progress slowed. The organization had become dependent on one person's translation skills. According to Harvard Business Review, making yourself indispensable "can tether you to your job and compromise your wellbeing." Worse, it blocks your team from developing the independence they need.

There's a particular exhaustion that comes from being the default fixer. It isn't just long hours. It's the mental weight of carrying decisions that belong to others. It's knowing that you've become the single point of failure.

## The Cost of Never Letting People Struggle

Without struggle, confidence never fully forms.

When you consistently step in, people stop taking risks. They stop experimenting. They wait for you to appear. Over time, they internalize a message you never intended to send: "You're not capable of handling this alone."

That's organizational atrophy disguised as efficiency.

## Starting With One Question

Change rarely begins with a sweeping philosophy. It started with one deceptively simple question: "What have you tried so far?"

That single prompt returned ownership to the person facing the problem. It signaled that initiative was expected. It also helped separate a skill gap from a confidence gap, which turns out to be crucial.

The next layer was the Three Asks Rule. Before offering any solution, ask three thoughtful questions to guide someone toward their own answer. By the third question, the path forward often becomes clear to them. When people arrive at solutions themselves, they take responsibility for the outcome in a way that no handed-down fix can replicate.

The urge to jump in never fully disappears. When you see someone struggling with a problem you could solve in minutes, pause and ask, "If I do nothing, what's the worst realistic outcome?"

Usually, the answer is minor delays or extra steps. If that's the cost of building real capability, it's worth paying.

## Capability Gaps Are Different From Confidence Gaps

Here's the distinction that changes everything: if someone lacks a skill, teach it or model it. If they have the skill but doubt themselves, stepping in reinforces the doubt. Restraint becomes the more powerful move.

The transition was uncomfortable. Some team members felt abandoned. Others questioned whether leadership was disengaged. But over time, collaboration increased. People started solving problems laterally instead of routing everything upward. When they did come to you, they arrived with clearer thinking and stronger proposals.

This is the difference between being the smartest person in the room and building a room full of people who can think for themselves.

## Leadership in an AI Era

As [Technology](https://infeeds.com/tags/?tag=technology) solves technical problems faster than humans ever could, the fixer trap is evolving. The temptation now is over-relying on tools or hoarding access to insights. AI can solve optimization problems, flag anomalies, and crunch data in seconds. What it can't do is develop judgment, intuition, or trust. It can't sense when someone needs encouragement instead of instruction.

Human leadership still matters. In fact, it matters more.

## The Real Test of Leadership

True leadership isn't about being the fastest problem solver in the room. It's not about being indispensable or being the person everyone turns to when things break. It's about creating an environment where others learn to think, decide, and lead without needing you to be present.

That's how [business](https://infeeds.com/tags/?tag=business) scales. That's how leaders stop being bottlenecks and start building something that lasts. The question isn't whether you can solve the next problem. It's whether your organization could solve it without you.
