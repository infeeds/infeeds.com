The United States and Saudi Arabia have inked a groundbreaking "peaceful nuclear cooperation agreement" that promises to reshape energy dynamics in the Middle East, but the fine print has sparked serious alarm among nuclear nonproliferation experts and lawmakers across the political spectrum.

Described by the US Department of Energy as providing "great access for American companies in the Saudi nuclear energy program," the deal could reportedly allow Saudi Arabia to enrich uranium on its own soil. That's a significant development because while enriched uranium fuels power plants, it can also be weaponized to create nuclear bombs. The actual text hasn't been released publicly, leaving much to interpretation.

## A Historic First with Serious Implications

What makes this deal truly unprecedented is that the US has never before allowed another country to conduct uranium enrichment domestically. Rosemary Kelanic, director of the Middle East programme at the Defense Priorities think tank, called it "quite shocking," explaining that domestic enrichment capabilities dramatically increase proliferation risks.

"If you can produce your own nuclear fuel, you become a much bigger risk for building nuclear weapons," she said. That concern isn't hypothetical. Back in 2018, Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman told CBS News that "without a doubt if Iran developed a nuclear bomb, we will follow suit as soon as possible."

The timing matters here. This agreement arrives amid escalating tensions between the US and Iran, with Saudi Arabia hosting multiple American military bases that have been targeted by Iranian attacks. Just two days before the announcement, Iranian-backed Houthis declared a "maritime blockade" against the kingdom. The geopolitical chess game is unmistakable.

## Congressional Battles Ahead

The deal now heads to Congress for review, where opposition is expected from lawmakers on both sides of the aisle, though Republicans currently control both chambers. Democratic Senator Edward Markey condemned the agreement with sharp language, accusing the Trump administration of "hypocrisy" for allowing Saudi Arabia to develop nuclear technology while simultaneously claiming to prevent Iranian nuclear advancement.

There's delicious irony here: Secretary of State Marco Rubio previously endorsed the "No Nuclear Weapons for Saudi Arabia Act" when he served as a senator. That law specifically required congressional approval for any Saudi nuclear deal. Now Rubio is assuring critics that "the US is not going to reach any agreement with any country that leads to the risk of proliferation." The contradiction hasn't escaped Democrats' notice.

What's particularly striking is that Saudi Arabia reportedly no longer needs to recognize Israel as a condition for the deal. Previous negotiations had hinged on that diplomatic requirement, but that demand appears to have quietly vanished.

## Regional Security and Commercial Interest

The agreement's commercial dimensions are enormous. American companies could profit substantially from building uranium enrichment facilities worth billions of dollars. Meanwhile, Russia and China have reportedly discussed similar arrangements with other nations, and US officials likely didn't want to be left behind in what could become a lucrative and strategically important market.

The 2009 US-UAE nuclear agreement offers a comparison point. The United Arab Emirates accepted strict limitations and doesn't produce its own fuel, accepting imported material instead. If Saudi Arabia gets enrichment rights, it would represent a significant departure from the nonproliferation standard that's been carefully maintained.

US Secretary of Energy Chris Wright attempted to assuage concerns, stating that "these agreements uphold the highest standards of nuclear safety and nonproliferation, while relying on the world's best nuclear technology and scientists, designed right here in the United States." Yet experts worry that decades-long partnerships can evolve unpredictably, and technological capabilities once shared are difficult to contain.

Israeli nuclear experts have voiced concern too, though some acknowledge that even with enrichment capabilities, developing a functional nuclear weapon would likely take years and face international resistance. Still, the trajectory matters more than the timeline.

This deal represents a strategic gamble: short-term alliance cultivation and commercial gain against the long-term risk of another major power entering the nuclear club in a volatile region already defined by sectarian tensions and proxy wars. The stakes couldn't be higher.
