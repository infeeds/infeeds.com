M&A activity exploded in 2025, with U.S. deal volume projected to hit $2.3 trillion. Founders are understandably excited. They've built something valuable. The buyer showed up with real money. The term sheet got signed. Now comes the victory lap, right?

Wrong. And the data isn't kind about this.

Research analyzing 40,000 mergers over four decades found that 70-75% of M&A deals fail to achieve their stated objectives. That's not a rounding error. That's a pattern. The term sheet you just signed doesn't guarantee smooth sailing—it's barely the opening act.

The real drama unfolds in the months after signature, when the assumptions you made collide with operational reality, investor expectations resurface, and your team realizes the new owner thinks very differently about how business should work.

## When Voting Rights Aren't Enough

Imagine this: You've built enough control to call the shots. You own the voting rights. Investors who wrote early checks are now minority holders. The deal moves forward on your terms.

Then they ask to renegotiate.

It's easy to say no when you have the leverage. But leverage and wisdom aren't the same thing. At InList, the founder faced exactly this situation. Past investors wanted to revisit terms despite being vastly outnumbered. The founder chose to work with them anyway—not because he had to, but because burning bridges in tight-knit industries rarely pays dividends.

The real lesson sits underneath: voting rights give you legal control, but relationships determine whether your merger creates lasting value or just lingering resentment. Even when you're holding the stronger hand, investors who wrote significant checks early deserve consideration. Not capitulation. Consideration.

That distinction matters more than most founders recognize.

## The Culture Collision Nobody Warns You About

The buyer looked good on paper. Aligned vision. Synergistic markets. Then implementation began.

The buyer's model was fundamentally different. InList made money on individual transaction fees. The new owner wanted to shift to membership-based revenue. That's not a tweak. That's a strategy reversal. Senior staff started quitting almost immediately. People who'd built their careers around the old model didn't want to rebuild around the new one.

Here's what the founder got wrong initially: he assumed his team would simply follow the new owner's lead. He didn't create space for honest feedback before the deal closed. He didn't anticipate how deeply the culture shift would hit.

Your team doesn't owe the buyer anything. They owe you, the person who hired them. If you're still around post-deal, that loyalty matters. Create real conversations early about what's changing. Let people tell you whether they can adapt or whether they're heading for the exits. The time to figure this out is before you sign, not after you've already committed.

## The Spiraling Legal Bill Nobody Anticipated

Lawyers love a complex transaction. Each new round of redlines generates fees. Each unresolved issue triggers another revision. Negotiations drag. Costs explode.

Kroger spent $684 million on merger-related costs in 2024 alone. While most transactions are substantially smaller, the cost pressure follows a similar pattern: you're paying lawyers to negotiate points that don't materially affect whether the deal succeeds.

Sometimes the best negotiation tactic is knowing when to let the other side win a minor point. Sometimes it's recognizing that another round of legal review costs money without buying protection. Set clear fee structures upfront. Build in checkpoints where you pause and ask: does this argument actually matter, or are we just burning money because we're stuck in negotiation mode?

## Your Post-Merger Role Is a Minefield

Many founders make a critical mistake here. You're staying on as a minority shareholder. Maybe you'll stick around during transition. Maybe you'll consult for a few months. You feel like you still have skin in the game.

That's when you expose yourself to maximum downside with minimal upside.

If you're signing personal guarantees, tying your compensation to metrics you can't fully control, or taking operational responsibility without clear boundaries, you've entered dangerous territory. The new owner can shift priorities. Market conditions can change. You're on the hook for outcomes you don't fully command.

Get everything in writing. Document your scope, your compensation structure, your exit triggers, and your liability limits. Future you will desperately thank present you for this clarity.

## The Messy Cap Table Solution

One founder inherited a nightmare: a former partner had promised equity to various contributors over the years. "A bit of equity here, some equity there." Now that mess landed on the current CEO's desk right before acquisition.

The solution was creative restructuring. Instead of bringing new partners into the existing InList Inc. entity (which would have required resolving every legacy equity commitment), the founder formed a new entity. InList Inc. and the new partners became co-owners of this new structure, with assets transferred accordingly. Nobody had to litigate who owned what piece of the original company.

That saved months of negotiation. If your cap table is a historical artifact of small equity grants, equity option pools, and handshake deals, consider whether similar structural creativity might unlock a path forward.

## Trust, But Verify

The buyer showed good faith in important moments. He started paying certain expenses before the deal officially closed. That gesture built trust. It signaled commitment. It also sent a message: this person has the resources and willingness to move things forward.

But trust and naivety aren't synonyms. The founder stayed prepared to pursue other options should the deal fall through. Trust is conditional. It's earned through consistent action, not given out generously at the start.

## The Final Compromises

Here's the uncomfortable truth: you won't get everything on your wish list. At some point, you have to cave on terms you personally felt were fair and necessary. The question is what you cave on.

The founder's guide was simple: do what's right for the business's shareholders. Whatever deal could be struck, that was the one worth seeing through. Not the maximum deal. Not the perfect deal. The best deal actually available.

That calculus requires letting go of ego. Some founders never learn this.

## Strategic Alignment Changes Everything

The buyer, Christian Jagodzinski, brought real experience from a successful exit in Germany. His social network for the elite shared natural synergies with InList's access to exclusive venues. That alignment mattered enormously. When operational friction emerged, there was something to fall back on: a genuine shared vision for where the combined business could go.

Strategic alignment doesn't erase challenges. But it makes them surmountable. Two parties pulling in different directions hit problems that feel insurmountable. Two parties with common objectives hit the same problems and solve them.

## What Actually Matters

The <a href="https://infeeds.com/tags/business">business</a> playbook for surviving M&A isn't mysterious. Build legal protections. Maintain relationships even when you have leverage. Communicate honestly with your team before the deal closes. Get creative about structural solutions. Be prepared for final terms to shift.

The initial term sheet is the beginning, not the end. Everything that follows requires navigating uncertainty, managing egos, protecting your downside, and accepting that the deal you thought you were making might not be the deal you end up living through.

The real question is whether you're prepared for that gap.
