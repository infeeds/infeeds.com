The United States and Saudi Arabia have inked a historic "peaceful nuclear cooperation agreement" that opens the door to the Gulf kingdom developing its own civilian nuclear program. The deal, announced after Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman's November visit to Washington, represents a significant shift in US nuclear policy and has already sparked fierce debate among lawmakers and experts.

## What the Deal Actually Covers

While the full text remains classified, US media reports suggest the agreement could allow Saudi Arabia to enrich uranium on its own soil for the first time. Instead of importing nuclear fuel, the kingdom could operate a US-built enrichment facility to produce fuel for power plants. Sounds straightforward enough, but here's where things get complicated: enriched uranium can fuel reactors or nuclear weapons.

The US Department of Energy insists these agreements "uphold the highest standards of nuclear safety and nonproliferation," adding that they advance both American and regional security interests. Energy Secretary Chris Wright emphasized that the technology comes "designed right here in the United States." Translation: this could be worth billions to American companies.

## Why This Matters

The timing is everything. This deal arrives as tensions between the US and Iran escalate dramatically, with Iranian-backed Houthis announcing a "maritime blockade" against Saudi Arabia just days before the announcement. Saudi Arabia hosts several US military bases that have been targeted by Iranian attacks, making the kingdom's strategic importance to Washington impossible to overstate.

But here's the kicker: Trump reportedly dropped a key demand that had stalled similar negotiations under previous administrations. Traditionally, the US insisted that any nuclear deal with Saudi Arabia required the kingdom to recognize Israel and normalize relations. That condition appears to have vanished.

## The Proliferation Problem

Rosemary Kelanic from the Defense Priorities think tank doesn't mince words. "The US has never done that before," she said about allowing uranium enrichment on foreign soil. "If you can produce your own nuclear fuel, you become a much bigger risk" for developing nuclear weapons.

Israeli officials have voiced serious concerns. So have nuclear nonproliferation experts. And they have a point: Saudi Arabia's Crown Prince himself told CBS News in 2018 that if Iran developed a nuclear bomb, "we will follow suit as soon as possible." That's not exactly a comforting assurance.

## Congressional Resistance

Even some Republicans are uncomfortable with this pivot. Massachusetts Senator Edward Markey condemned the deal as reckless hypocrisy, pointing out that Secretary of State Marco Rubio once endorsed the "No Nuclear Weapons for Saudi Arabia Act" when serving as a senator. Now Rubio claims the US would never strike any agreement risking nuclear proliferation. The irony isn't lost on critics.

The agreement heads to Congress for review, though Trump's party controls both chambers. Still, opposition from both sides of the aisle suggests this won't be a rubber-stamp approval.

## The Bigger Picture

Why now? One theory circulates quietly in policy circles: Russia and China have reportedly been discussing similar nuclear arrangements, and the US feared being left out of lucrative energy sector partnerships. Better to act first than watch competitors grab influence and contracts.

This deal also fundamentally differs from a 2009 agreement the US struck with the UAE, where the Emirates agreed not to produce their own fuel and accepted stricter limitations. That comparison underscores just how permissive this new arrangement appears to be.

The agreement could reshape Middle East power dynamics, though any actual weapons development remains years away. Yet the legal and technological foundation is being laid now. In a region already tense with [news](https://infeeds.com/tags/news) about Iran, Israel, and proxy conflicts, allowing Saudi Arabia to build indigenous uranium enrichment capacity feels like betting the house on stability that may not last.

The fundamental question remains unanswered: can America really trust that technology meant for civilian power plants won't eventually serve military purposes?
